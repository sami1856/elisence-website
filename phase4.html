Phase 4 · Stability, Integrity & Comparative Intelligence Layer
From App to Health Infrastructure

Phase 4 transforms Elisence from a single application into a governance-grade, audit-ready health intelligence infrastructure for ministries, hospitals and regulators.

It is the layer where **every number, model and comparison is traceable**. WORM audit logs, FHIR interoperability, advanced provenance and AKAC privacy controls ensure that real-world evidence is safe, verifiable and compatible with national healthcare standards across the UK, EU, US and GCC regions.

Stable · Auditable · Privacy-First · Comparative Intelligence

1. Vision – Why Phase 4 Exists

Phase 4 establishes the foundation where governments, clinicians and citizens can trust Elisence’s conclusions at **national scale**. It guarantees that:

  • All real-world evidence (RWE) is traceable from input to output.
  • Every analytical result is backed by immutable logs and QA.
  • Privacy and clinical safety are enforced by design, not by policy only.

This is the layer that prepares Elisence for **formal evaluation** by ministries of health, national health services and global partners.

2. Core Engine – Four Technical Pillars

WORM Ledger · FHIR Interoperability · Provenance Engine · AKAC

2.1 WORM Ledger — Immutable Audit Trails

A cryptographically enforced, append-only ledger that stores:

  • Dataset fingerprints and hashes.
  • Model version identifiers and configuration snapshots.
  • QA digests and full test outcomes.
  • Export hashes, signatures and delivery timestamps.
  • Archive and snapshot timestamps.

Why it matters: regulators and hospitals can reconstruct exactly how a conclusion was produced. There are **no silent edits**, no hidden changes, and no trust gaps.

2.2 FHIR Integration — Global Interoperability

Phase 4 harmonises medication and usage data with global medical standards:

  • FHIR resources: Medication, MedicationRequest, MedicationStatement.
  • RxNorm / ATC codes for drug identification.
  • MedDRA for adverse event and side-effect coding.
  • SNOMED CT / ICD-10 for conditions and diagnoses.

Why it matters: ministries, hospitals and health systems can integrate Elisence without rebuilding their infrastructure. FHIR makes Elisence **“plug-into-the-health-system ready.”**

2. Core Engine (continued) – Provenance & AKAC

2.3 Provenance Engine — End-to-End Traceability

Every analysis, chart, comparison or export carries embedded provenance metadata:

  • Data sources and ingestion paths.
  • Transformations, filters and quality checks applied.
  • Model parameters, training lineage and runtime versions.
  • Timestamps, environment and execution context.
  • Digital signatures, QA status and reviewer information.
  • AKAC privacy level and cohort size used.

This provides true real-world evidence transparency, aligned with UK / EU / US / GCC expectations for medical data and pharmacovigilance.

2.4 AKAC — Adaptive Knowledge Access Control

AKAC is a dynamic privacy supervisor that:

  • Adjusts anonymisation thresholds based on cohort size.
  • Applies k-anonymity and safe aggregation rules.
  • Blocks unsafe slices, drill-downs or high-risk combinations.
  • Ensures aggregate reports cannot deanonymise individuals.

Why it matters: citizens gain confidence that their identity is protected; regulators gain formal proof of compliance; clinicians still receive **useful, safe insights**.

3. Intelligence & Analytics

Phase 4 houses the analytical brain of Elisence, converting raw usage data into trusted insight for **public health, clinical practice and national decision-making**.

3.1 Drug Effectiveness Intelligence

The engine computes, with QA and provenance on each metric:

  • Mean effectiveness metrics and improvement scores.
  • Response rates across cohorts and sub-populations.
  • Δ-change metrics (before / after treatment windows).
  • Net Benefit Index (NBI) combining benefit and safety.
  • Cohort-specific performance curves over time.

3.2 Side-Effect Intelligence

Integrated safety analytics using:

  • MedDRA-coded adverse events with severity grading.
  • Proportional Reporting Ratios (PRR) and ROR calculations.
  • Temporal patterns – onset, duration and recurrence.
  • Geo-demographic risk variations and clusters.

This produces contextualised safety profiles for public health and for clinician governance boards.

3. Comparative & Global Medical Intelligence

3.3 Comparative Intelligence Engine

The flagship capability of Phase 4, enabling:

  • Drug-vs-drug comparisons across matched cohorts.
  • Cohort matching and propensity-score weighting.
  • Benefit–risk scoring, ranking and scenario exploration.
  • Multilingual narrative generation (EN / FA / AR / TR / RO) explaining results in plain language.

Narrative example:
“Drug B showed substantially higher effectiveness and fewer severe adverse events than Drug A in this cohort, based on anonymised real-world data.”

This allows clinicians and ministries to compare therapeutic options **without guesswork or black-box behaviour**.

3.4 Global Medical-Intelligence Layer

A unified intelligence fabric that powers:

  • National health and utilisation reports.
  • Population-level analytics and trend detection.
  • Ministry dashboards and oversight tools.
  • High-level, non-diagnostic risk–benefit summaries for citizens.

This layer turns Elisence from an app into a **real health-system intelligence backbone**.

4. Safety, Governance & Compliance

4.1 QA Gates – No Output Without Evidence

No analytical output is released unless it passes a chain of QA gates:

  • Completeness and coverage checks.
  • Recency and freshness of data.
  • Duplicate and outlier detection.
  • Internal consistency and plausibility checks.
  • Cohort safety and minimum-size thresholds.
  • Privacy and AKAC compliance checks.

If any gate fails → the output is **blocked, flagged and not shown** to end users.

4.2 Governance of Effectiveness & Adverse Intelligence

  • Transparent, documented statistical methodology.
  • Versioned models with full changelog and review trail.
  • Clear statement of assumptions and known limitations.
  • Clinical-grade metrics and validation procedures.
  • Multilingual narratives for global clinical and policy use.

5. Compliance, Impact & Scalability
