Mental Health & AI — Why Safety Must Come Before Automation

By Saami Salami Asl — Founder of Elisence

1. Mental Health Is Not a Place for Experiments

When someone searches for support in a difficult moment, they are not looking for a “cool new AI”. They are looking for:

  • calm, clear information
  • non-judgemental guidance
  • reassurance that they are safe and not alone

This is why mental health AI must never be treated as an experiment on real people.

2. Automation Without Safety Is a Risk

Pure automation can:

  • misunderstand context
  • respond too quickly with the wrong tone
  • create false certainty where nuance is needed
  • miss signs of serious risk

In mental health, these mistakes are not minor bugs — they can be deeply harmful.

3. What “Safety First” Actually Means

A safety-first mental health system should:

  • stay clearly non-diagnostic and non-prescriptive
  • encourage users to speak with real clinicians when needed
  • avoid making promises it cannot keep
  • have strict boundaries for what it will and will not say

4. How Elisence Approaches Mental Health

In Elisence, mental health features are designed around:

  • a calm, supportive tone — not panic or pressure
  • gentle prompts to breathe, pause and reflect
  • clear signposting to professional help and crisis lines
  • Zero-Trust controls for who can see which data

The goal is to be a companion, not a replacement for real clinical care.

5. Privacy and Dignity as Core Rules

Mental health data is among the most sensitive information a person can share. This is why Elisence uses:

  • strict access controls and consent mechanisms
  • WORM audit logs for high-risk access
  • data minimisation for dashboards and exports
  • clear separation between wellness support and clinical pathways

6. The Future of Mental Health AI

The future will not belong to the loudest or fastest AI, but to the systems that:

  • protect users before impressing them
  • prioritise safety over automation
  • remain honest about their limits
  • earn the trust of clinicians, regulators and families

Elisence is built on that belief: in mental health, safety must always come before automation.
