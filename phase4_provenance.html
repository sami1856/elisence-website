Phase 4 · Provenance & Integrity Engine
Provenance + Integrity

This page explains the trust layer behind Phase 4: how Elisence proves that every KPI, chart, and exported insight is reproducible, policy-bound, and audit-ready under BBVP and Zero-Trust.

Phase 4 is designed for situations where evidence must stand up to independent review: ministries, hospitals, regulators, research partners, and governance boards. The system answers one question with absolute clarity: "Can we trust these outputs, and can we prove how they were produced?"

The result is an evidence-grade engine where every output can be traced back to a controlled input set, a versioned rule/policy pack, deterministic analytics, documented QA gates, and immutable audit records. No silent edits. No hidden assumptions. No untraceable numbers.

1. What “Provenance” Means in Phase 4

In Elisence, provenance is not a document or a promise. It is a machine-enforced chain of custody for every analytic outcome. For each KPI or exported bundle, Phase 4 records:

  • Input fingerprints (dataset hashes, cohort definitions, time windows).
  • Transformation lineage (filters, normalisation rules, aggregation steps).
  • Method identity (metric definitions, statistical rules, computation mode).
  • Policy context (country policy pack, privacy thresholds, AKAC decisions).
  • Runtime identity (engine version, configuration snapshot, environment context).
  • QA evidence (quality-gate results, test digests, acceptance checks).
  • Export proof (manifest, signature, delivery timestamp, archive reference).

Principle: If an output cannot be proven, it must not be shipped.

2. What “Integrity” Means in Phase 4

Integrity is the guarantee that no one can quietly change evidence after the fact. Phase 4 achieves this with immutable records, cryptographic fingerprints, and strict release gates.

Integrity is enforced at four levels:

  • Data integrity: inputs are hashed; cohort slices are policy-bound; unsafe cohorts are blocked.
  • Computation integrity: deterministic analytics; versioned rules; reproducible runs.
  • Evidence integrity: manifests, signatures, and immutable audit references for exports.
  • Operational integrity: BBVP test discipline, no skips, no warnings ignored, no silent fallbacks.

For ministries and regulators, this means the analytics layer can be reviewed like an engineering system: the same inputs and versions must reproduce the same outputs, and any change leaves an audit trace.

3. QA Gates – No Output Without Evidence

Phase 4 does not treat quality as a dashboard feature. It treats quality as a release condition. Before an output can be displayed or exported, it must pass a chain of gates.

Typical gate families include:

  • Completeness: required fields present; expected coverage; missingness thresholds.
  • Consistency: internal plausibility checks; bounds and range validation; unit consistency.
  • Freshness: time window checks; stale data rejection; recency policies.
  • Cohort safety: minimum cohort size; safe aggregation rules; k-anonymity thresholds.
  • Policy compliance: country rules; AKAC access decisions; allowed drill-down restrictions.
  • Release discipline: test suites; evidence generation; manifest updates.

Gate rule: if any QA gate fails, the output is blocked and cannot be exported as evidence.

4. WORM Audit – Immutable History

WORM (Write Once, Read Many) is the backbone of Phase 4 auditability. It is an append-only record that makes post-hoc tampering detectable. Phase 4 stores audit events such as:

  • Data ingests, dataset fingerprints, and execution timestamps.
  • Quality gate outcomes (pass/fail) with deterministic identifiers.
  • Export events: manifest hash, signature reference, delivery metadata.
  • Policy decisions that affected output visibility (e.g., blocked cohort slices).
  • Version changes: engine version, policy pack version, schema version.

This matters because institutional evidence must survive scrutiny months later: auditors should be able to reconstruct what was done, when, under which policy and version.

WORM posture: append-only, integrity-first, reconstructable, reviewable.

5. Manifests, Hashes, and Signatures

Phase 4 exports are not “files”. They are evidence bundles. Every bundle includes a manifest that lists:

  • Each included file path.
  • Each file SHA-256 hash.
  • Bundle metadata: policy version, engine version, schema version, timestamps.
  • QA digest: which gates ran and their results.
  • Export identity: bundle ID and audit references.

Optionally, bundles can be signed. The signature allows external reviewers to validate that the evidence was produced by a known release process and was not modified after export.

Export rule: a bundle without a manifest is not an evidence export.

6. AKAC and Privacy-First Analytics

Phase 4 is population-level and non-diagnostic. It must protect citizens from re-identification risk while still producing useful evidence for institutions. This is enforced through policy-bound access and AKAC-style controls.

The system enforces:

  • Safe aggregation: no unsafe small cohorts; no risky combinations.
  • Policy isolation: outputs are tied to a policy pack version (country/region).
  • Release restrictions: drill-downs are blocked if they increase re-identification risk.
  • Export discipline: what can be exported is explicitly governed and logged.

For ministries and hospitals, this produces evidence that is defensible. For citizens, it ensures analytics cannot expose identity through slicing.

7. BBVP – Build, Back, Verify, Prove

Phase 4 is built under BBVP discipline: every capability is implemented cleanly, then backed by tests, verified through deterministic runs, and proven via evidence pack outputs. This is what separates a demo dashboard from a governance-grade engine.

Phase 4 proof discipline typically includes:

  • Unit tests for schemas, policies, validators, and gates.
  • Integration tests for analytics outputs and export manifests.
  • OFF/ON behaviour validation for feature flags.
  • Evidence pack snapshots that can be re-validated externally.

Operational rule: no skipped tests and no ignored warnings in release paths.

8. Who Uses This and How

Ministries and Regulators

  • Review population-level KPIs and trend intelligence under strict privacy policies.
  • Validate the provenance trail for any published KPI or comparison.
  • Audit export manifests and confirm evidence integrity.

Hospitals and Governance Boards

  • Inspect safety and outcome signals at service and cohort level.
  • Use policy-bound exports for governance discussions and reporting.
  • Trace every metric back to its evidence chain when challenged.

Universities and Research Partners

  • Consume synthetic or anonymised evidence bundles for methodological review.
  • Re-run validations against the manifest and reproduce published outcomes.
  • Compare cohorts with documented assumptions and QA gates.

This engine is built to support institutional review: clear artefacts, reproducible outputs, and defensible integrity posture across regions (UK/EU/US/GCC).
