Interview — The Vision Behind Elisence

In conversation with Saami (Sami) Salami, founder of the Elisence global health intelligence ecosystem

Interviewee: Saami (Sami) Salami · Founder of Elisence
Topic: Why Elisence exists and how the 17-phase ecosystem fits together.

1. Why was Elisence created?

Q: If you had to explain in one sentence why Elisence exists, what would you say?

A: Elisence exists because families, clinics, and ministries need health information that is safe, clear, and honest. I wanted a system that supports people in their daily lives without pretending to be a doctor, and that gives regulators the transparency they always wished digital health platforms had.

Q: Was there a personal moment that pushed you to start this?

A: Yes. Over the years I experienced how fragile trust can be when information is incomplete or unfair. Complex legal processes, misunderstandings, and investigations showed me what happens when systems don’t keep good records and don’t explain themselves clearly. Elisence is my answer to that: a platform where every decision, every rule, and every boundary is traceable.

2. What makes Elisence different from a typical health app?

Q: Many apps say they improve health. What is different about Elisence?

A: Most apps start from features. Elisence starts from governance. From day one we designed:

• clear non-diagnostic, non-prescriptive boundaries
• Zero-Trust security — every request must prove who it is and what it is allowed to see
• WORM-style audit logs so that history cannot be silently rewritten
• a multi-phase roadmap that treats families, women, diabetes, nutrition, mental health, and ministries as one connected ecosystem, not separate products

Q: So Elisence is not a replacement for doctors?

A: Absolutely not. Elisence is designed to be a companion and intelligence layer, not a doctor. We help people understand patterns, risks, and habits in a safe way, and we help clinics and ministries see the bigger picture — but medical decisions remain with qualified professionals and approved guidelines.

3. How do the 17+ phases fit together?

Q: You often talk about 17+ phases. What does that mean in practice?

A: Think of Elisence as a city instead of a single building. Each phase is a “district” with its own purpose: Women+ Health (Phase 7), Diabetes & GLP-1 (Phase 9), Messaging & Public Profiles (Phase 10), Family Ecosystem 0–18 (Phase 15), Global Nutrition Intelligence – GNIE (Phase 16), and many more. They all share the same security, audit, and governance backbone.

Q: Why build so many phases instead of one big super-app?

A: Because health is not one topic. A woman’s cycle, a child’s sleep, a father’s diabetes, and a country’s nutrition policy are deeply connected but they need different tools and safety rules. The ecosystem approach lets us design each phase with the right boundaries while still reusing data responsibly when families or ministries need a full picture.

4. Safety, trust, and Zero-Trust security

Q: You mention “Zero-Trust” very often. How does that shape Elisence?

A: Zero-Trust means we never assume that a device, a user, or an internal service is safe just because it is “inside the system”. Every request has to prove: who it is (identity), what it wants to do (scope), and why it is allowed (policy). For users this is invisible — it just feels like a smooth experience — but for regulators and auditors it means there is a clear, verifiable chain of evidence.

Q: How do you avoid scaring normal users with all this security language?

A: The rule is simple: complexity for the system, simplicity for the person. On the surface, users see friendly cards, clear messages, and supportive language. Under the surface, we keep the strict controls: AKAC-style privacy supervision, WORM logs, k-anonymity thresholds, and so on. Families should feel cared for, not interrogated.

5. What does success look like for Elisence?

Q: When you imagine Elisence five or ten years from now, what do you see?

A: I see families who feel less alone with health questions. I see ministries that finally have trusted, auditable data without invading privacy. I see clinics using Elisence as a safe bridge between real life and medical records — not a replacement, but a translator.

Q: What is your personal measure of success?

A: For me personally, success is when Elisence becomes a place where my daughter, and millions of other children, can grow up with better information, kinder technology, and more honest systems than the ones we went through.
