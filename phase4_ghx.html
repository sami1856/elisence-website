Phase 4 · GHX – Governance & Health Exchange
GHX

GHX is the interoperability and governance exchange layer that turns Phase 4 analytics into integration-ready, policy-bound evidence packages for real health systems.

Phase 4 produces trusted, privacy-safe population evidence. GHX is the part that makes those outputs exportable, auditable, and usable by ministries, hospitals, regulators, and research institutions — without breaking Zero-Trust boundaries.

In practical terms, GHX defines how Elisence delivers “evidence bundles” with manifests, hashes, structured metadata, and clear integration contracts. It is designed for national-scale workflows: review → approval → export → integration → audit.

1. What GHX Is

GHX stands for Governance & Health Exchange. It is not a dashboard and not a single API. It is the export-and-integration layer that sits above Phase 4 evidence and turns it into standards-ready packages.

GHX exists to answer:

  • How do we export analytics in a way that is traceable and audit-proof?
  • How do we ensure each export is policy-bound (country/region rules) and cannot be misused?
  • How do we make exports interoperable with national health infrastructures?
  • How can an external reviewer validate integrity without trusting internal systems blindly?

GHX posture: exchange evidence, not identities. Share packages, not raw user records.

2. Who Uses GHX

Ministries and National Regulators

  • Receive policy-grade KPI bundles for population monitoring and programme oversight.
  • Require manifests, hashes, and audit references to validate integrity.
  • Use GHX exports for governance, procurement evaluation, and national reporting.

Hospitals and Health Networks

  • Consume governance-ready evidence packs for service monitoring and safety oversight.
  • Integrate population insights with existing EHR ecosystems via standards mapping.
  • Run internal audits using exported manifests and audit trails.

Universities and Research Partners

  • Validate methodology through reproducible exports and documented assumptions.
  • Use synthetic or anonymised evidence packs for evaluation and publications.
  • Re-check integrity by verifying hashes and bundle manifests.

3. Evidence Bundles – What Gets Exported

GHX exports are bundles with machine-verifiable integrity. A typical bundle can include:

  • Aggregate KPIs and trend series (population-level only).
  • Metadata: policy version, engine version, schema version, time window.
  • Provenance summary: how the outputs were derived (non-identifying).
  • QA evidence: which gates ran and whether they passed.
  • WORM audit references: identifiers that point to immutable history.
  • Export manifest: file list + SHA-256 hashes.
  • Optional signature references for external validation.

Export principle: a KPI without a manifest is not an evidence export.

4. Interoperability – Standards and Mapping

GHX is built to make evidence exchange realistic for health systems that already use structured standards. It supports a mapping layer for key health constructs.

Typical interoperability concepts include:

  • FHIR-aligned packaging where appropriate for medication and programme reporting structures.
  • Code mapping (e.g., medication coding, adverse-event coding) to reduce integration friction.
  • Controlled schemas with versioning so integrations do not break unexpectedly.
  • Policy isolation: exports remain tied to the source policy pack and region rules.

GHX is designed to be integration-ready without exposing raw patient-level identifiers. It supports governance workflows and standards expectations while remaining non-diagnostic.

5. Governance – Policy-Bound Exchange

GHX enforces that evidence exchange cannot bypass privacy or policy. Exports are generated under:

  • Country/region policy packs (rules, thresholds, export boundaries).
  • Cohort safety (minimum sizes, safe aggregation, blocked slices).
  • Access-tier rules (who can export and what level of detail is permitted).
  • Audit trace for each export decision and delivery event.

Zero-Trust rule: export decisions must be explainable and auditable, not discretionary.

6. Audit Trails – How External Validation Works

GHX is built for environments where external reviewers must validate evidence without relying on internal trust. Typical validation steps:

  • Verify the bundle manifest and SHA-256 hashes match the files received.
  • Confirm the policy version and engine version match the declared release.
  • Check QA evidence and gate results included in the bundle.
  • Use WORM audit references to confirm immutable export history exists.

Integrity workflow: verify package → verify manifest → confirm policy/version → confirm audit references.

7. Why GHX Matters

Many digital health projects fail not because analytics are weak, but because evidence cannot be shipped safely into real institutional workflows. GHX exists to remove that barrier.

GHX provides:

  • Defensible evidence exchange for ministry-grade adoption.
  • Integration readiness for hospitals and health networks.
  • Audit-friendly packaging for regulators and governance boards.
  • Stable versioning so integrations can evolve safely over time.

In short: Phase 4 makes analytics trustworthy. GHX makes that trust deliverable to the real world.

Final Statement

GHX is the bridge between evidence and real-world adoption. It packages Phase 4 outputs into governance-grade evidence bundles that can be integrated, reviewed, and audited — while preserving Zero-Trust boundaries and population-only safety posture.
